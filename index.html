Heading In

The Emerald Ash Borer

Agrilus planipennis
  • Invasive beetle from Asia, arrived in North America ~2002
  • Has killed billions of ash trees across the continent
  • The fallen trees open the canopy…
  • …which actually benefits spring wildflowers by letting in light

Multiflora Rose

Rosa multiflora
  • Native to Asia — the USDA actively encouraged planting it in the mid-1900s as erosion control and "living fences"
  • Now one of the most invasive shrubs in eastern North America; a Federal Noxious Weed
  • Each plant produces up to 500,000 seeds per year, spread by birds
  • Dense thickets are completely impenetrable — has taken over large sections of the woods
  • Cutting it just stimulates regrowth; it is extremely hard to eradicate
  • Wasn't like this when I was a kid.

Morel Mushrooms

Morchella spp.
  • Emerge in spring when soil temps reach ~50°F
  • Love dying ash, elm, and old apple trees
  • Notoriously elusive — even experienced hunters get skunked
  • Hollow inside

What Are Spring Ephemerals?

  • Wildflowers that complete their entire above-ground life in just weeks
  • Emerge, flower, set seed, and vanish — before the tree canopy leafs out
  • Racing to capture sunlight through the narrow spring window
  • By summer, the forest floor looks like they were never there

My grandparents' woods are full of them. Let's take a tour.

Prairie Trillium

Trillium recurvatum
  • Everything in threes: 3 leaves, 3 petals, 3 sepals
  • Sepals reflex (fold back) — hence recurvatum
  • Deep maroon petals; native to the Midwest
  • Takes 7–10 years to bloom from seed
  • Never pick one — the plant may not recover for years

Spotted a few still waking up — just leaves, no flower yet:

Trout Lily

Erythronium americanum
  • Named for its mottled brown-and-green leaves — just like a brook trout
  • The nodding yellow flower closes up at night
  • Plants don't flower until they're ~7 years old
  • A colony of thousands may have only a handful of flowers
  • Seeds are dispersed by ants (myrmecochory)

Wild Blue Phlox

Phlox divaricata
  • Also called "woodland phlox" — fragrant lavender-blue flowers
  • One of the best early-season nectar sources for pollinators
  • Unlike true ephemerals, its foliage lingers through summer
  • Often found growing through leaf litter on rich woodland floors

Virginia Bluebells

Mertensia virginica
  • A true spring ephemeral — completely gone by June
  • Buds open pink, then turn blue as they mature (pH shift in cell sap)
  • Loves rich bottomland woods along rivers and streams
  • One of the most spectacular spring wildflowers in eastern North America

May Apples

Podophyllum peltatum
  • These "umbrellas" push up through the leaf litter each spring
  • Only two-leaved plants flower — single-leaved ones are juveniles
  • The white flower hides beneath the leaves
  • Everything is toxic… except the ripe yellow fruit in late summer
  • A cancer drug (Etoposide) is derived from this plant

Marsh Marigold

Caltha palustris
  • One of the earliest spring bloomers — sometimes while snow lingers
  • Those brilliant yellow "petals" are actually sepals — no true petals
  • Grows in seeps, stream margins, and wet woodland edges
  • Historically eaten as a spring green — must be cooked first (raw is toxic)

Jack-in-the-Pulpit

Arisaema triphyllum
  • The "pulpit" = the spathe; "Jack" = the spadix inside
  • Tiny flowers are hidden at the base of the spadix
  • Plants change sex from year to year — bigger plants become female
  • Fooled flies enter the pulpit briefly, picking up pollen on the way out
  • Berries turn brilliant red in fall… and are toxic
  • All parts contain burning calcium oxalate crystals

Pawpaw

Asimina triloba  ·  "the Indiana Banana"
  • The largest edible fruit native to North America
  • The only non-tropical species in its genus — a piece of the tropics hiding in a midwestern forest
  • Tastes like a cross between banana, mango, and vanilla custard
  • Grows in the forest understory, often in clonal colonies via root suckers
  • Blooms in early spring — small dark maroon flowers that mimic rotting meat to attract fly pollinators
  • The Zebra Swallowtail butterfly depends entirely on pawpaw as its larval host plant
  • A very short shelf life keeps them out of grocery stores — you have to find them yourself

Black Locust

Robinia pseudoacacia
  • Those thorns can be 4–5+ inches long — serious business
  • Native to the Appalachians; naturalized across the Midwest for hundreds of years
  • Why such enormous thorns? Probably defense against Pleistocene megafauna — mastodons, giant ground sloths — that went extinct ~10,000 years ago
  • An "evolutionary anachronism": the tree hasn't gotten the memo that its predators are gone
  • Fixes nitrogen like a legume — alters the soil chemistry around it
  • Extremely rot-resistant wood; fence posts last for decades
